About This Project

Giant pumpkins, some over 2,500 pounds, are the heaviest fruit in the world and extreme examples of human-driven evolution. The One Ton Genome Project will analyze the DNA of record-breaking pumpkins to see how selection has reshaped their genetic makeup. This research allows us to observe evolution at work and uncover how extreme traits emerge, with insights for crop and livestock breeding, and evolutionary biology.

Context

Giant pumpkins are extraordinary examples of human-driven evolution through artificial selection. Over the past fifty years, growers have carefully bred and saved seeds from their largest fruits, increasing pumpkin size from a few hundred pounds to more than 2,500 pounds and dramatically altering plant growth traits.

Giant pumpkins are derived from Cucurbita maxima, a domesticated squash species with many cultivated varieties. Previous studies identified genetic differences between large and small-fruited pumpkins , but have not examined diversity within the competitive lineages maintained by giant pumpkin growers. By sequencing the DNA of historic and modern lines from growers, we can track the genetic changes behind the quest to grow the largest fruit on Earth. Standing on the shoulders of these giants, we can also gain insight into the evolutionary process that gave rise to all of our domestic plants and animals.

Significance

“Believing that it is always best to study some special group, I have, after deliberation, taken up domestic pigeons.” – Charles Darwin, On the Origin of Species

Scientific insight often comes from unexpected places. Unlike studies of ancient domestication, competitive giant pumpkin lineages are exceptionally well documented: pedigrees are recorded and seeds from record-breaking fruits preserved. This creates a rare opportunity to study evolution under strong directional selection.

Long-term selection experiments, such as the Illinois maize selection program for oil and protein, demonstrate that populations can continue responding to selection for many generations, yet the genetic mechanisms underlying sustained progress remain poorly understood.

This research will provide insight into how extreme traits arise and the genetic changes underlying sustained artificial selection. Beyond pumpkins, these insights are broadly relevant to crop improvement and evolutionary biology.

Goals

Working with growers, we have obtained pumpkin seeds from the 1980s to today, including historic record-breakers. We will extract DNA and sequence their genomes to reconstruct how the giant pumpkin lineage has changed. Select cultivars and wild Cucurbita maxima relatives will be included in analysis to provide a baseline for evaluating giant pumpkin diversity.

By comparing genomes across decades, we will quantify how genetic diversity has changed during fifty years of intense selection for fruit weight. These analyses will reveal whether meaningful genetic variation still exists within giant pumpkins—shedding light on the balance between genetics and environment in producing the world’s largest fruits and offering a rare view of how genomes change under sustained artificial selection.

Budget

A total of 96 giant pumpkin samples will be processed at approximately $100 per sample, which covers DNA extraction, library preparation, and sequencing (approximately 4-5 million reads per sample). This dataset will allow us to quantify genetic diversity within giant pumpkins, identify regions of the genome consistently favored by selection, and reconstruct relationships among influential breeding lines.

If funding exceeds the initial goal, we will expand the project to sequence up to 192 samples, providing an even more comprehensive picture of genetic variation within giant pumpkins. If funding allows, we will generate a high-quality reference genome for the Atlantic Giant using long-read PacBio sequencing. This genome will provide a foundation for future studies and improve our ability to interpret the genetic basis of extreme fruit size.

Andy Wolf

Andy Wolf has been chasing record-breaking giant pumpkins since 1999 in Little Valley, New York, constantly pushing the limits of fruit weight. He has generously shared his expertise and provided historic seeds and guidance to support the One Ton Genome Project.
